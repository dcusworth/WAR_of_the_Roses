Project Overview

Reality TV has become a staple of modern popular culture. ABC's popular TV show The Bachelor garners millions of viewers each week. Even under the guise of "reality" and "true love", we hypothesize that there exist underlying patterns that in general enhance a contestant's chance of winning. To simulate this "game of love", we create a dynamic model that is updated each week of the competition that predicts contestant's chance of winning.

Background

The Bachelor is a competition based reality TV show. Each season, about 30 women vie for a wedding proposal of a single Bachelor. Each week several women are eliminated until one contestant wins. To determine which contestants stay and which are eliminated, each week the Bachelor selects only a few contestant's to accompany him on a one-on-one or group date. At the end of the episode, there is a cocktail party where the Bachelor meets with any contestants who were not given a date during the episode. Finally, the episode closes with a rose ceremony where the Bachelor chooses who will proceed to the next episode by extending to them a rose.

Objective

We analyze underlying features of all winning and losing contestants on the The Bachelor. Using these data, we build a statistical model to predict week-by-week success on the TV show.

Approach Source ABC.com

Data Sources:

Wikipedia: A scrape of Wikipedia provided us fundamental contestant data, specifically each contestant's age, hometown, and profession. Secondly, we scraped data pertinent to the competition aspect of the TV show, which tell us whether for a given week a contestant received a one-on-one date, a group date, or a "won" a group date by receiving an early rose.

Twitter: We ran several searches around the airdate of each episode of The Bachelor to retrieve all tweets that pertained to a particular contestant for a particular episode.

Studio photographs: Using the Wayback Machine, we scraped ABC's website to retrieve all studio photographs of each contestant of The Bachelor.

Models:

Logistic Regression: We only have complete datasets (Wikipedia + Twitter + Photographs) for Seasons 13-19 of The Bachelor. For these seasons, we fit a unique logistic classifier to predict their probability of advancing to the next round. We use the following features in the prediction model:

Features in Logistic Classifier Age Difference from Bachelor Hometown Distance from Bachelor's Hometown Hometown FCC Region Profession Type Twitter Sentiment from Previous Week Percentage of all Tweets from Previous Week Received a Group Date in Previous Week Won Group Date in Previous Week Received a One-on-One Date in Previous Week PCA of Face

Each week a new logistic classifier was fit to the data. We used a 70% testing/training split of the data, and optimized the model using L-1 or a "LASSO" penalty. Each classifier's optimal hyper-parameter was optimized using a grid search cross validation.

Clustering by Profession:

We used the International Standard Classification of Occupations (ISCO) to cluster each contestant by their profession. We performed this clustering by doing approximate matching of each contestant's given profession to a list of ISCO occupations. A contestant was classified according to whichever category gave the most matches to a contestant's profession. Below we plot a bar chart which shows the distribution over seasons 13-19 of the contestants' different ISCO profession types. In blue we plot the distribution of the winners. We see that most contestants fall in the Managers, Professionals, Technicians, and Service industries.

Tweets:

Twitter Sentiment: For each episode for each contestant, we look at all tweets. Using Python's natural language processing toolkit (NLTK), we extract all the adjectives + adverbs from each tweet. We then take advantage of a sentiment analysis done on a corpus from movie reviews and tweets provided through an API at text-processing.com. Then for each tweet, we compute it's positivity probability based on the average positivity of all its adjectives and adverbs. Twitter Share: In addition to sentiment, we recorded the total number of tweets that a contestant received per episode, and then computed the percentage of contestant to total tweets per episode.

Conclusion

The number of tweets a contestant received the week before an episode aired effectively predicted whether the contestant would be eliminated from the show in that episode. Our linear regression model indicated that 'tweet_share', the variable for a contestant's number of tweets during the previous week, had a very high coefficient for all weeks. The other variables' coefficients were consistently near zero.

Below is a histogram of results for Week 6, indicating that the coefficient for 'tweet_share' is near 25.

However, the FCC economic area group of bachelor and contestant hometowns were not effective predictor variables for which contestant would not be eliminated from an episode. In our linear regression models for each week of the Seasons, the FCC economic area group coefficient was near zero.

We ran a linear regression models for each week, for all Seasons, to check whether the distances between the Bachelor's hometown and the Contestants' hometowns were effective predictors of who would not be eliminated. For each week, the coefficient for the distance variable was almost zero. Distance between hometowns is not an important factor for predicting True Love.

We made several statistical models to assess which factors best predicted winning contestants. First, we ran linear regression models using the wikipedia background and twitter data as variables, to predict which contestants would not be eliminated. We made one model for each week of the Season, for all Seasons. So, for example, the model for "week 1" consisted of week 1 data for Seasons 13 to 19.
